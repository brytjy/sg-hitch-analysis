SINGAPORE - Just $8 for a Gojek ride from Woodlands to Bugis, $9 for a ride from Marsiling to Changi and $10 for a direct ride from Dhoby Ghaut to Tampines on a Friday evening.

These prices, which could be up to 75 per cent cheaper than official fares set by ride-hailing company Gojek, are what many passengers across Singapore are believed to have been paying for their rides in the past two months.

This is how it works:

Passengers join the SG Hitch telegram messaging group, and post carpooling requests with details of their journeys in hope that drivers would respond.

But "middlemen" would respond to these posts, offering to make Gojek bookings for the passengers at a discounted price.

After the Gojek ride is completed, the passenger would make a payment usually via PayNow to the middlemen who in turn pay Gojek the official fares.

Gojek drivers receive the full commission from the non-discounted fare, making the arrangement sounds like a win-win.

But passengers could have been unwittingly benefiting off someone's stolen credit card, or have even become part of a money laundering scheme, according to experts The Straits Times spoke to.

Gojek said it is investigating the suspected scam and would be submitting a police report on the matter.

But further checks found that at least two dozen middlemen have been providing these Gojek bookings for hundreds of passengers in the past month, judging by the postings in the SG Hitch telegram group.

The online chat group was initially set up for drivers and passengers to list carpooling requests, and now has about 18,000 members, with as many as 1,800 trip requests daily.

Posing as a passenger, this reporter posted 11 trip requests, which drew responses from 25 middlemen.

The cost savings from such rides went as high as $30, such as the journey from Woodlands to Tampines, after surge pricing was taken into account.

SINGAPORE - Just $8 for a Gojek ride from Woodlands to Bugis, $9 for a ride from Marsiling to Changi and $10 for a direct ride from Dhoby Ghaut to Tampines on a Friday evening.

These prices, which could be up to 75 per cent cheaper than official fares set by ride-hailing company Gojek, are what many passengers across Singapore are believed to have been paying for their rides in the past two months.

This is how it works:

Passengers join the SG Hitch telegram messaging group, and post carpooling requests with details of their journeys in hope that drivers would respond.

But "middlemen" would respond to these posts, offering to make Gojek bookings for the passengers at a discounted price.

After the Gojek ride is completed, the passenger would make a payment usually via PayNow to the middlemen who in turn pay Gojek the official fares.

Gojek drivers receive the full commission from the non-discounted fare, making the arrangement sounds like a win-win.

But passengers could have been unwittingly benefiting off someone's stolen credit card, or have even become part of a money laundering scheme, according to experts The Straits Times spoke to.

Gojek said it is investigating the suspected scam and would be submitting a police report on the matter.

A compromise of the Gojek app has also been mooted as a possible reason behind the unusually cheap rides.

These possible scenarios could lead to jail time and fines for the middlemen, said a criminal lawyer, who added that whether passengers could be criminally liable too is in a grey area.

In a report by The Straits Times last month, Gojek had referred to such discounted bookings as suspected scams as some riders ended up paying more than what was advertised by the middlemen.

But further checks found that at least two dozen middlemen have been providing these Gojek bookings for hundreds of passengers in the past month, judging by the postings in the SG Hitch telegram group.

The online chat group was initially set up for drivers and passengers to list carpooling requests, and now has about 18,000 members, with as many as 1,800 trip requests daily.

Posing as a passenger, this reporter posted 11 trip requests, which drew responses from 25 middlemen.

The cost savings from such rides went as high as $30, such as the journey from Woodlands to Tampines, after surge pricing was taken into account.

Many of the middlemen, who used local mobile numbers, would become uncontactable soon after making the bookings, while some even deleted their accounts.

ST understands that the middlemen would handle the payment to Gojek through credit or debit cards.

Mr Alan Lim, Asia-Pacific practice leader at IBM Blockchain Labs, said these cases could involve credit card fraud.

But he added that ride-hailing providers have previously reported such schemes where unsanctioned middlemen have used stolen credit card credentials to exploit the inability of these companies to verify the correct users of their platforms.

Nanyang Business School Adjunct Associate Professor Zafar Momin also flagged the bookings as possible credit card fraud.

"Credit card companies and banks may need to compensate the affected consumers; ride-hailing operators like Gojek obviously have the scammed fares reversed and may still need to compensate the drivers, not to mention the fallout of potential reputational risk," he added.

Security experts at Trustwave said money laundering could also be involved.
